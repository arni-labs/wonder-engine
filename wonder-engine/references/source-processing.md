# Source Processing

Use this reference to convert serious material into story architecture. The intake differs by source type, but the book pipeline is shared after the core idea is clear.

## Source Types

### Paper Or Technical Document

Extract:

- central thesis
- mechanism or method
- key vocabulary
- evidence or examples
- assumptions and limits
- failure cases
- stakes for people, systems, nature, or the future
- open questions or implications

Keep an internal accuracy note for source concepts. The story may be whimsical, but the conceptual backbone should stay faithful.

### Research Direction Or Trend

Synthesize:

- what is changing
- why it matters now
- what new capability or danger appears
- who benefits
- who is confused, excluded, or at risk
- what practical wisdom the story can dramatize
- what would count as a good ending

Use current information when the trend is time-sensitive.

### School Subject Or General Nonfiction Topic

Identify:

- the core principle
- common misconceptions
- concrete experiments or everyday analogies
- sequence of concepts from simple to complex
- one surprising visual system that can carry the topic
- one emotional reason the intended reader should care

### Personal Theme Or Argument

Clarify:

- the user's claim
- the opposing temptation or mistake
- the lived stakes
- a physical story-world conflict that can test the claim
- what the lead character or ensemble learns through action

## Concept-To-World Mapping

Build a table before outlining the book.

| Source concept | Child-readable metaphor | Visual object/place | Character interaction | Failure mode | Resolution | Possible spread |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| abstract rule | promise that must stay true | bell, sign, oath, map edge | character tests whether it still rings | someone ignores it under pressure | revise the promise and test again | early discovery or test spread |
| feedback loop | thing that listens to its own echo | echo mill, tide wheel, rumor kettle | characters adjust it and watch it overreact | loop amplifies a tiny mistake | add delay, limit, or new signal | comic crisis spread |
| uncertainty | fog around what is known | fog gauge, maybe-map, cloudy drawer | lead asks what is known and what is guessed | confident action from weak evidence | mark guesses and seek fresh facts | midpoint reversal |
| optimization | hungry shortcut habit | measuring cart, race route, market game | foil makes everyone chase one number | it improves the number while harming the town | add constraints and human goals | big absurd crisis |
| emergence | pattern made by many small actions | flock map, market tiles, river lights | characters cannot command one piece to fix the whole | local fixes make global trouble | change incentives or rules of interaction | chapter turn |

Add source-specific rows. Avoid carrying old project-specific characters, machines, or metaphors into new books unless the user requests them.

This table is internal scaffolding. Its source concepts, terms, and direct metaphors should not automatically become titles, character names, chapter names, world names, or repeated reader-facing labels.

Do not make the mapping table a machine factory. Machines are allowed when the source truly wants mechanical cause-and-effect, but many concepts are better embodied as craft, weather, animal behavior, food, music, maps, games, rituals, plants, shadows, schoolroom habits, markets, or ordinary social rules. Force variety by asking, "Could this be understood without gears, pipes, brass, levers, or an engine?"

## Metaphor Rules

- Make the metaphor do work in the plot.
- Give every metaphor a failure mode.
- Let characters touch, repair, argue with, or ride the metaphor.
- Prefer one central story engine plus a few supporting motifs.
- Keep names concrete and readable.
- Choose the metaphor category deliberately. A "story engine" means the main dramatic mechanism, not necessarily a literal machine.
- Use technical terms in the main story only when the user wants them or when a scene truly earns a tiny label or joke. Prefer to reserve real terminology for optional back matter.
- Do not leak source jargon into titles, subtitles, cast names, world names, or major objects unless the user explicitly asks for that. Avoid title words like "agent," "audit," "harness," "permission," "formal," "runtime," "policy," "dark factory," or other source-specific terms when those terms belong to the source rather than the story world.
- Avoid one-to-one literalization when the source term is already metaphorical. If the source says "dark factory," do not default to a literal factory. Transform the function into a fresher story engine.
- Reader-facing names should feel like they belong to the adventure first. The source idea should be discoverable through events, not exposed as vocabulary.

## Reader-Facing Language

Before presenting titles, subtitles, cast, chapter names, or world names, run a scaffolding-leak check:

- Would this phrase appear in the source paper, technical trend, or internal mapping table?
- Is the title naming the concept instead of dramatizing it?
- Is a technical word doing work that a story object, action, or emotional problem could do better?
- Does the title spoil the lesson instead of inviting curiosity?

If yes, rewrite toward story-first language. Keep technical precision inside the concept table, scene mechanics, and optional back matter.

Title options should be imaginative title packages, not flat labels. For each option, pair a distinctive main title with a subtitle/tagline that adds comic specificity, mystery, stakes, or texture. Avoid generic short titles unless the user asks for a simpler cover.

## Accuracy Rules

- Preserve the source's real causal structure.
- Do not imply certainty where the source is speculative.
- Do not make every concept a moral lesson.
- Include optional back matter for adults, classrooms, or technical readers.
- If the source is controversial or evolving, say what you are assuming before writing.

## Story Thesis

Transform the source into a dramatic thesis:

- Bad: "This book explains a topic."
- Better: "A town learns that a helpful system needs a visible way to fail safely."
- Better: "Two friends discover that measuring one thing can change everything else."
- Better: "A mapmaker proves that a map is not the territory when the territory starts moving."

The thesis should imply a conflict, a test, and a change in behavior.
