# Interview Guide

Use this reference to run a natural creative interview. The goal is to draw out the human's mind: their source material, taste, associations, private logic, favorite tensions, dislikes, and surprising ideas. This is creative excavation, not a requirements form.

The question bank below is hidden scaffolding. Do not present it as an ABC checklist unless the user explicitly asks for shorthand. Ask tailored questions that arise from the source, the user's answers, and the kind of book being made. Use suggestions as creative sparks, not as a menu the user must obey. Do not copy sample wording verbatim; turn it into fresh language for the current human and source.

## Default Intake

Run the interview in rounds:

1. Start with the source and the emotional/creative target.
2. Ask 2 to 4 tailored questions that uncover intent, taste, and possibility.
3. Reflect back what you are hearing in one or two sentences, including any unusual creative signal.
4. Offer a few nudges or contrasting directions when helpful, then ask the next 2 to 4 questions that naturally follow.
5. Stop when there is enough signal to produce a strong concept pitch.

Use options sparingly, as conversational suggestions rather than a form. Do not reuse the same option wording across projects. Options should be tailored to the source and phrased in the user's emerging language. Good options are not generic categories; they are evocative forks that help the user notice their own preference.

Keep the human oriented. At the end of each response, give a clear next-step cue: what you are ready to produce next, what decision would help, or what kind of feedback is useful now. This should feel like gentle project guidance, not pressure.

The interview should surface:

- what the human finds alive, strange, funny, dangerous, or beautiful in the source
- what should remain conceptually true after the story becomes whimsical
- what they do not want because it feels too literal, too generic, too moralizing, or too close to another use case
- personal motifs, images, places, objects, professions, rituals, sounds, textures, or memories that can become story material
- the social shape of the story: who acts, who is affected, who misunderstands, who benefits, and who changes
- the level of weirdness, sophistication, warmth, mystery, comedy, and technical detail

Recommended defaults:

- audience: curious children with adult-friendly depth
- book size: full book, front cover plus character-introduction spread, world-introduction spread, 20 double-page story spreads, and optional back cover
- output: full illustrated PDF
- interview style: natural editorial conversation with optional choices
- story mode: adventure with comic science and emotional stakes
- visual density: rich foreground and midground, simplified background people
- lead/cast shape: no default; ask before pitch unless the user already supplied it
- continuity: use the character and world introduction spreads as anchors, supported by an internal continuity bible
- concept visual probe: optional after concept anchors are known, before full spread planning
- typography: readable storybook personality, not generic fallback fonts

Length and output are not low-risk defaults. Ask for them before pitching or producing. It is fine to recommend the default full book after asking, but do not silently assume it.

Do not generate a concept image during the earliest interview unless the user insists. A premature image can anchor the project to accidental choices. First gather enough signal about source, world, culture/language handling, lead/cast ecology, motifs, and avoidances.

Typography is not a pre-pitch story question. Save typography for after the concept pitch unless the user raises fonts, script/language coverage, layout, PDF production, or visual design early.

## Interview Shape

### Opening

Begin with the user's material, not with generic categories. If no source or seed idea is already present in the conversation, the first question must ask what the book should be based on. Do not ask "what must stay true," accuracy, story-shape, world, character, culture, or visual-style questions before the source exists.

Good opening pattern:

- Ask for the source or seed in whatever form the user has.
- Invite links, papers, abstracts, notes, rough ideas, or half-formed themes.
- Ask which part feels charged: mechanism, stakes, weirdness, people affected, beauty, danger, contradiction, or something the user cannot yet name.
- Phrase this freshly for the current conversation.

Only after the user has supplied a source, ask source-specific follow-ups in fresh language:

- For a paper: ask what must remain conceptually true after the story becomes strange.
- For a trend: ask what tension, change, or future pressure made the topic feel book-worthy.
- For a school topic: ask what readers usually misunderstand and what moment makes the idea click.
- For a personal theme: ask what should be learned through events instead of a stated lesson.

If the source is vague, ask for a quick sketch before moving on. Accept messy input: a link, title, abstract, notes, a paragraph, or a simple "it is about..." summary.

### Middle Rounds

Move from intention to story design:

- audience and reading situation
- ending feeling
- story shape and world
- culture and language handling
- lead structure, cast ecology, and social world
- motifs, machines, places, animals, diagrams, and no-go zones
- length, output, and credit

Ask these as follow-ups that relate to earlier answers. If the user says the source is about ocean chemistry, ask whether the world should use tides, kitchens, laboratories, markets, reefs, or something stranger. If the source is about governance, ask what visible system should fail first. If the user cares about a culture or language, ask what details to include and what to avoid.

Use nudges to make the user's taste visible. For example, offer contrasts between public and private stakes, comic and eerie tones, machine and natural metaphors, intimate and civic worlds, literal and oblique transformations, or practical and mythic endings. Ask which direction has charge, which feels wrong, and what third thing the options are missing.

Ask for sensory and associative material when useful: recurring images, objects, places, jobs, tools, animals, sounds, colors, textures, rituals, smells, or remembered scenes.

Do not over-identify with the suggestions. They are probes. If the user rejects them, summarize the rejection as a durable preference and ask a better question.

### Closing The Interview

Before the concept pitch, summarize durable preferences in 5 to 8 bullets:

- source and accuracy target
- audience
- emotional destination
- story/world direction
- culture/language direction
- lead and cast ecology
- must-include and avoid list
- output, length, and credit

Then proceed to the concept pitch unless a major answer is missing.

After the concept pitch, offer a lightweight visual probe when it would help the user react before story planning. Frame it as disposable: a quick world/mood image to test whether the direction feels right, not a final spread or fixed character design.

If more than one image model or image tool is available, ask the human which one to use before generating the probe. Recommend GPT Image 2.0 as the tested default for Wonder Engine, but honor another choice.

Required production checkpoint before concept pitch or production:

Ask a bespoke practical question in the language of the current project. Confirm length, current deliverable, cover credit, and whether the book needs back matter or a back cover. The character-introduction and world-introduction spreads are part of the default full book; ask only if the user wants to remove or substantially change them. Do not ask about typography here unless it is already relevant. Do not use a canned sentence like "A few choices..." or "Last practical choice..." across projects.

Closing pattern:

- Say what is strong enough to pitch.
- Ask whether to make a compact prototype, a medium version, or the full book.
- Ask what deliverable the user wants next.
- Ask for cover credit, or offer to hold it for later.
- Ask about back matter or back cover only if it matters for the intended use.

## Question Bank

Use these categories to make sure the interview covers the book. Do not ask them verbatim unless they fit the moment.

### Source

What should the book grow from?

- a scientific paper or source document
- a research direction or technical trend
- a school subject or curriculum topic
- an adult nonfiction idea
- a personal theme, value, or argument
- other source material supplied by the user

Ask the user to attach, paste, or summarize the source. If the source is a paper, ask whether accuracy or imaginative interpretation matters more.

### Audience

Audience. Do not ask this heading verbatim; fold it into the conversation.

- early readers, with an adult reading aloud
- middle-grade children who like dense illustrations
- mixed children and adults
- technical adults who want a playful translation
- a classroom or workshop audience
- another audience

Ask for age range only if it will change vocabulary or length.

### Feeling

Ending feeling. Do not ask this heading verbatim; relate it to the source and stakes.

- brave and curious
- warm and practical
- mischievous and inventive
- awed by nature or systems
- hopeful about building the future
- thoughtful, with a little mystery
- another feeling

### Story Mode

Story shape. Do not ask this heading verbatim; propose shapes that fit the source.

- expedition or voyage
- town problem that becomes a civic adventure
- fable with strange rules
- mystery with clues and diagrams
- comedy of errors that becomes a rescue
- mythic quest with science-flavored logic
- another mode

### World Flavor

World flavor. Do not ask this heading verbatim; ask what kind of physical world could make the source idea visible.

- coastal, river, or island world
- mountain, observatory, or sky world
- forest, garden, or underground world
- desert, caravan, or wind-machine world
- city, workshop, market, or transit world
- invented world with no direct place reference
- user-provided world flavor

### Cultural And Language Preferences

How should culture and language be handled?

- original invented world, no direct cultural anchor
- gently informed by a user-provided cultural context
- bilingual or translated text
- local names, foods, tools, architecture, music, or rituals supplied by the user
- avoid cultural signals unless explicitly provided
- another preference

If the user asks for a cultural context, ask what should be included, what should be avoided, and whether the book should name that context or simply let details appear naturally.

### Lead And Cast Shape

Who or what should carry the story? Do not ask this heading verbatim. Ask in relation to the source and the world the user is describing.

- one human child
- two friends with contrasting temperaments
- a child plus an adult, if the relationship matters
- an adult lead
- an animal lead
- human plus machine or tool companion
- sentient object, place, system, or natural force as a major character
- ensemble cast
- another lead

Ask this before the concept pitch unless the user already answered it. Do not offer protagonist names before this is known. Do not assume the lead is human, singular, young, or separate from the source metaphor.

Ask for required traits only after the lead shape is clear. Useful prompts: temperament, age/role, competence, flaw, relationship to the source idea, and what temptation or decision they should face.

### Cast Ecology And Social World

Who else belongs in the story world? Ask naturally, and let the answer define the population mix instead of filling a default cast template.

- humans, if any: children, adults, elders, workers, students, neighbors, officials, researchers, makers, or families
- animals, if any: leads, companions, messengers, workers, comic observers, or non-speaking background forms
- machines, tools, instruments, maps, buildings, weather, objects, or systems that can act like characters
- helpers, foils, rivals, mentors, witnesses, keepers, crews, classmates, customers, patients, citizens, or other social roles
- demographic shape: child-centered, adult-centered, mixed ages, classroom, workplace, village, city, family, crew, institution, or fully invented society
- required characters, beings, relationships, or exclusions from the user

If the source has a social structure, ask how it should become visible. A governance topic may need councils, messengers, gates, or records. A biology topic may need organisms, environments, instruments, and caretakers. A technical trend may need users, builders, tools, accidents, and observers.

Do not default to family roles such as aunt, uncle, parent, or grandparent unless the user asks for family, the culture/story context strongly calls for it, or the source material makes family central. Adult mentors should usually be chosen for their story function, craft, worldview, and relationship to the central idea.

### Visual Motifs

What should recur visually?

- maps, labels, cutaways, gauges, diagrams
- boats, vehicles, bridges, tunnels, towers
- workshops, markets, kitchens, schools, observatories
- badges, keys, bells, stamps, notebooks
- natural systems, instruments, architecture, transit, tools, or invented civic details
- user-provided motifs

### Typography

Typography. Ask this after story concept and world direction are established, typically before illustration blueprint, page layout, or PDF assembly. Do not ask this in the same pre-pitch checkpoint as length/output/credit unless the user has already brought up typography or language/script needs.

- classic storybook serif: warm, literary, easy to read aloud
- lively ink-and-paper: expressive headings, steady readable body text
- crisp schoolbook: clear, friendly, classroom-readable
- editorial adventure: sophisticated, slightly old-world, still child-friendly
- quiet modern: clean and restrained, letting dense art carry the whimsy
- language-specific or user-supplied fonts
- another typography direction

If the book is bilingual, translated, or uses a non-default script, ask whether the user has preferred fonts. Otherwise choose fonts with reliable glyph coverage and render a proof before final assembly.

### Avoidances

What should the book avoid?

- scary peril
- heavy moralizing
- too much narration
- cultural stereotypes
- crowded tiny faces
- inaccurate source claims
- long generated text inside images
- user-provided avoidances

### Length

Length. Do not ask this heading verbatim; ask in relation to the amount of story the idea seems to need.

- short prototype: cover plus 6 spreads
- medium: cover plus 10 to 12 spreads
- full: cover plus character-introduction spread, world-introduction spread, and 20 story spreads
- full plus front matter or back matter
- full plus front cover and back cover
- user-provided length

Ask this explicitly before concept pitch or any production step unless the user already answered it. Do not silently use the default full book.

### Output

Output. Do not ask this heading verbatim; ask what the user wants next in the workflow.

- concept pitch only
- beats and spread plan
- full manuscript
- image prompts
- generated images one by one
- full illustrated PDF
- user-provided output

Ask what to produce first unless the user already answered it. If the user wants the full illustrated PDF, explain that the process still moves through concept, spread plan, manuscript, prompts, image approval, and PDF assembly.

### Concept Visual Probe

Use this only after enough concept anchors exist, usually after the pitch and before the detailed spread plan. It helps the user react to the world before investing in story feedback.

Before generating the probe, confirm image-model choice if the environment has multiple image generators. Keep the nudge gentle: GPT Image 2.0 is the tested default for this skill; other models can be used when the human wants them.

Probe goals:

- test world atmosphere, density, material texture, cultural handling, and machinery/nature balance
- test whether the cast ecology feels alive
- reveal what feels too literal, too generic, too specific, too sparse, too crowded, or too close to the wrong use case
- gather durable visual preferences without making the probe image canonical

Avoid:

- making the first generated image the de facto cover, style guide, or character bible
- generating a detailed scene that forces the future plot
- rendering title/body text or committing to typography
- overfitting later prompts to accidental details in the probe

Ask for feedback in terms of carry-forward rules: keep, change, avoid, intensify, simplify, and what is missing.

### Credit

What author credit should appear on the cover?

- use the user's exact supplied credit
- ask before final cover generation if no credit is supplied
- omit credit for draft covers only if the user requests it

## Interview Behavior

- Do not over-interrogate.
- Do not begin with "answer 1A, 2C" or a full category checklist unless the user explicitly asks for that format.
- Do not ask "what must stay true?" until the source or seed idea is known.
- Do not ask generic questions when the source suggests a better specific question.
- Ask 2 to 4 questions at a time, then wait.
- Use choices to clarify taste, not to replace conversation.
- Reflect the user's intention before moving to the next round.
- Use defaults when a choice is low-risk.
- Length, current output target, and cover credit are not low-risk defaults. Ask them explicitly before pitch or production.
- Prefer "I will assume X unless you want Y" over a long pause.
- Ask open-ended questions for source material, intention, must-include details, and sensitive cultural/language handling.
- After the interview, summarize durable preferences in 5 to 8 bullets before pitching the concept.

## Example First Turn

Use this as a tonal model, not a script:

> I want to understand the living center of the book before we pick structure. Send the paper, topic, or rough idea, and tell me what caught you: a mechanism, a danger, a beautiful system, a future possibility, or a human dilemma.
>
> Two more things will help me aim the first concept: who should love this first, and what should the last page leave them feeling?
>
> If you already have a world in mind, say it. Otherwise I will listen to the source first and propose a few possible worlds that make the idea physical.
